Website blockers have a bit of an image problem.

They are often treated like tools for people trying to squeeze every drop of output
from the day, or for anyone who thinks the answer to distraction is to make the
internet as joyless as possible. That is not how most people actually use them.

In real life, a good website blocker is less about intensity and more about relief.
It helps you remove the things that keep hijacking your attention so you can stay
with what you meant to do.

That can look different depending on the person.

## Professionals whose work lives in the browser

For a lot of people, work does not happen in a quiet document anymore. It happens in a
browser full of tabs, notifications, research, messages, videos, dashboards, and all
the usual traps sitting right beside the thing you are meant to finish.

If that is your day, a website blocker is not about becoming superhuman. It is about
making your work environment a little less slippery.

Sometimes that means blocking the sites you check on autopilot. Sometimes it means
putting those blocks on a schedule so your focus hours stay protected. And sometimes
it means cleaning up a page instead of blocking it entirely, because you still need
the useful part.

## Students and self-learners

Studying online can be genuinely hard because the same browser window that holds your
lecture notes also holds entertainment, group chats, and a thousand ways to drift.

That is where a blocker can help in a very practical way. It reduces the number of
tiny decisions you have to keep making when your energy is already low.

You do not have to keep renegotiating whether a quick scroll is fine. You do not have
to rely on perfect discipline. You just set up a little structure so the assignment,
reading, or tutorial in front of you has a better chance of holding your attention.

## People with ADHD who want more support, not more pressure

For people with ADHD, the internet can feel especially sticky. The problem is not a
lack of care or effort. It is that many online spaces are built to interrupt,
redirect, and reward instant attention shifts.

That does not mean a tool like Prohibeo is a treatment or a fix. It is not. But it
can be practical support.

Reducing feeds, recommendations, sidebars, and easy detours can make the browser feel
less loud. Blocking certain sites during the hours that matter can remove some of the
in-the-moment friction of trying to self-regulate in an environment that is designed
to pull you away.

For many people, that kind of support is not about becoming perfectly focused. It is
just about making momentum easier to keep once it finally shows up.

## People who want a quieter internet

Not everyone needs a website blocker because of work or school. Some people are just
tired of how grabby the web feels.

They want to watch one useful YouTube video without falling into recommendations. They
want to check something quickly without getting pulled into comments, feeds, or side
quests. They want a browser that feels a little more intentional and a little less
like it is always trying to sell them another click.

That is a completely valid reason to use a blocker.

Sometimes the best use of a tool like this is not extreme discipline. It is simply
creating an online space that feels calmer, cleaner, and easier to be in.

## The right blocker should fit your life

The most useful setup is rarely the strictest one. It is the one that matches how you
actually live and work.

For some people, that means blocking a handful of sites during work hours. For others,
it means keeping YouTube available for learning but removing the parts that pull them
into a loop. For others, it means adding just enough friction to stop every moment of
boredom from turning into a scroll.
