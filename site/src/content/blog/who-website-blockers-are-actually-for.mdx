Website blockers have an image problem.

They get framed as tools for productivity maximalists - people trying to squeeze every
last drop of output from the day.

That is not how most people use them.

A good blocker is less about intensity and more about relief. It removes the parts of
the browser that keep taking over your attention.

## People whose work happens in Chrome

For a lot of professionals, work lives inside a browser full of tabs, messages,
research, dashboards, docs, and easy detours.

In that setup, a blocker is not about becoming superhuman. It is about making the work
surface less slippery.

That can mean blocking the sites you check on autopilot, putting them on a schedule,
or cleaning up mixed-use sites so the useful part stays usable.

## Students and self-learners

Online study is hard for a simple reason: the same window that holds your notes also
holds entertainment, chats, and every possible side quest.

The value of a blocker here is not punishment. It is fewer decisions. You stop
renegotiating every quick scroll and give the assignment in front of you a fair chance
to hold your attention.

## People with ADHD who want support, not pressure

For people with ADHD, the web can feel especially sticky. Many online spaces are built
to interrupt, redirect, and reward instant attention shifts.

That does not make a blocker a treatment. It does make it useful support.

Reducing feeds, recommendations, sidebars, and other fast detours can make the browser
feel less loud. A scheduled block can remove some of the in-the-moment strain of
trying to self-regulate inside an environment designed to pull you away.

## People who are simply tired of a grabby internet

Not everyone uses a blocker for work or study.

Some people just want to watch one useful YouTube video, read one article, or check
one site without being dragged into the next thing. That is reason enough.

Sometimes the best use of a blocker is not strict discipline. It is a cleaner, quieter
browser.

## The right blocker fits your real life

The best setup is rarely the strictest one. It is the one you can keep.

For some people, that means blocking a few sites during work hours. For others, it
means keeping YouTube available for learning while hiding the recommendation loop. For
others, it means adding just enough friction to stop boredom from turning into a
scroll.
