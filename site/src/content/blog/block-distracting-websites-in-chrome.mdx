Blocking distracting websites in Chrome sounds easy until you try to do it for real.

You block a few obvious sites. Then you hit the problem: the internet is not only
where distraction lives. It is also where your work happens.

You may need YouTube for a tutorial, Reddit for a troubleshooting thread, or social
platforms for publishing and research. If you block everything too aggressively, the
system starts fighting your actual workflow.

The fix is precision.

## Split sites into three groups

Most distracting sites fit one of these buckets:

- hard stops: sites that almost never help and usually cost you time
- scheduled sites: sites that become a problem during work hours, not all day
- mixed-use sites: sites you need, but only after the noisy parts are removed

That simple split makes setup much easier.

If a site is mostly a trap, block it completely. If it only becomes a problem at
certain hours, schedule it. If the site is useful but the feed is the problem, clean
up the page instead of banning it.

## Use full blocks for obvious time sinks

Some sites do not need a nuanced strategy.

If you open a site almost entirely out of habit, and it rarely improves the task in
front of you, a full block is the cleanest choice. You remove the debate and move on.

This works best for sites that are consistent time drains rather than occasional tools.

## Use schedules for predictable weak spots

Many distractions are time-based.

Maybe your attention slips after lunch. Maybe work hours are the issue and evenings
are fine. Maybe weekdays need structure and weekends do not.

Scheduled blocking helps because the decision gets made before your energy drops. You
do not keep asking whether one quick check is okay. The rule is already there.

## Clean the page when blocking is too much

Often the problem is not the whole site. It is the part trying to keep you there.

Recommendations, comments, sidebars, autoplay, home feeds, and suggested posts can
turn a useful visit into a detour. Remove those, and the same site becomes much easier
to use with intent.

This is especially useful for mixed-use sites like YouTube, where the lesson may be
helpful even if the surrounding page is a mess. If that is your main struggle, read
[how to use YouTube for learning without getting pulled into recommendations](/blog/use-youtube-for-learning-without-recommendations/).

## Start smaller than you think

The best blocking setup is not the strictest one. It is the one you can keep.

Start with a short list:

- block two or three obvious time sinks
- add one schedule for your most important focus window
- clean up one mixed-use site you still need

Run that for a week. If it helps, keep it. If it gets in the way, adjust it.

Good focus tools should reduce friction, not create a second job.
