Blocking distracting websites sounds simple until you actually try to do it.

At first, it seems like the answer is obvious: block the sites that waste your time
and move on. But after a day or two, most people run into the same problem. The
internet is not just where distractions live. It is also where your work happens.

You might need YouTube for a tutorial, social platforms for publishing or customer
research, and news or forums for context. If you block everything too aggressively,
your focus system starts fighting your real workflow. When that happens, you either
disable it, work around it, or stop trusting it altogether.

A better approach is to block with precision.

## Start with what actually derails you

Not every distracting site needs the same treatment.

Some websites are pure time sinks for you. Those are good candidates for full
blocking. If visiting them almost never helps you do what you intended to do, make
the decision once and remove the daily debate.

Other sites are mixed-use. They are not always a problem, but they become a problem
in certain moods, hours, or tasks. Those usually respond better to limits that are
more selective.

The goal is to stop the patterns that repeatedly break your attention, not to create
unnecessary obstacles for yourself.

## Use schedules instead of relying on willpower

A lot of distraction happens at predictable times.

Maybe your focus dips in the late afternoon. Maybe you do your best work in the
morning and lose momentum after lunch. Maybe certain sites are only a problem during
work hours, not at night or on weekends.

That is where scheduled blocking becomes useful. Instead of asking yourself over and
over whether "just a quick check" is fine, you set the rule in advance. During your
focus window, the decision is already made.

This tends to work better than trying to be disciplined in the moment, especially
when you are tired, stressed, or avoiding a difficult task.

## Block less, remove more

Sometimes the problem is not the whole website. It is the part of the page designed
to keep you scrolling.

Recommendations, trending panels, suggested posts, sidebars, autoplay prompts, and
endless feeds are often what turn a useful visit into a distracted one. If you can
remove those attention traps, the same site becomes much easier to use intentionally.

That matters because many websites are not entirely good or bad. They are just built
to compete for as much of your attention as possible. Cleaning up the page can be
more effective than banning it outright.

## Make your rules easy to live with

The best blocking setup is not the strictest one. It is the one you can keep using.

That usually means:

- blocking the sites that are rarely worth visiting
- scheduling the ones that mainly hurt focus during work time
- reducing distracting page elements where full blocking would be too much
- adjusting as you notice what actually helps

If your system is too rigid, you will resent it. If it is too loose, it will not
help. The sweet spot is a setup that protects your attention without interrupting the
work you opened Chrome to do in the first place.
